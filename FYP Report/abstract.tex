\chapter*{Abstract}

Randomized algorithms are a cornerstone of modern theoretical computer science \cite{motwani1995randomized}, yet their proofs often rely on informal probabilistic intuition that obscures critical assumptions about independence and measurability.
This report presents a formalization and machine-checked proof of the Isolation Lemma, a key tool in randomized algorithms that isolates a unique minimum-weight solution by randomly assigning weights to elements in combinatorial problems such as the Perfect Matching problem \cite{mulmuley1987matching,lovasz1979determinants}.
By carrying out this development in the Isabelle/HOL interactive theorem prover \cite{nipkow2002isabelle,isabelleprob}, we make all probabilistic assumptions explicit and machine-checkable, thereby strengthening the rigor of the underlying algorithmic reasoning.

We begin by formally specifying the Isolation Lemma, including the underlying product probability space for random weight assignments, the precise statement of the lemma, and the combinatorial constructions required to reason about minimizers over finite sets.
Building on this foundation, we develop a complete machine-checked proof by synthesizing and reconciling arguments scattered across multiple informal and folklore-style presentations of the lemma and resolving implicit assumptions present in their informal treatments.
Finally, we apply the formalized Isolation Lemma within a two-stage machine-checked correctness argument for a randomized parallel algorithm for the Perfect Matching problem:
it first establishes a conditional correctness result for extracting the unique minimum-weight perfect matching, and then uses the formalized Isolation Lemma to justify the required uniqueness premise with high probability, thereby illustrating how such randomized arguments can be integrated into a rigorous formal verification framework.
