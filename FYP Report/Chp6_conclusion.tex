\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{chp:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Research Achievements}
This report has presented a comprehensive formal verification of a Randomized Parallel Matching algorithm, centered around a machine-checked proof of the Isolation Lemma in Isabelle/HOL.
By navigating the transition from pen-and-paper probabilistic intuition to structured formal logic, we have established a rigorous foundation for reasoning about randomized algorithmic constructions.

The primary contributions of this work are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rigorous Foundation for Isolation}:
    We constructed a complete product probability space and proved the $1 - |U|/N$ success bound, making all assumptions about measurability and independence explicit.
    \item \textbf{Algorithmic Correctness}:
    We successfully applied the Isolation Lemma to verify the "Weight-to-Multiplicity" bridge and the parallel edge extraction logic using the Tutte matrix and its minors.
    \item \textbf{Bridging the Formalization Gap}:
    We employed a layered PP/SP/FP approach to connect informal folklore-style proofs with fully formal machine-checked code.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodological Lessons and Reflections}
\label{sec:lessons}

The process of formalizing the Isolation Lemma and its application to the Perfect Matching problem has yielded several methodological insights into the nature of randomized algorithm verification.

\subsection{The Ontology of Randomness: From Intuition to PMF Objects}
A primary reflection from the modeling phase in Chapter \ref{chp:modeling} is that randomness must be treated as a first-class mathematical object rather than an implicit environmental property.
In classical RNC analysis, probabilistic intuition often obscures subtle dependencies.
By explicitly constructing product probability spaces using the \texttt{Pi\_pmf} constructor, we transitioned from "vague" randomized processes to machine-verifiable measures.
This rigor is essential to ensure that every assumption about independence is logically grounded.

\subsection{Structural Bridging: The Utility of the SP Layer}
As demonstrated in Chapter \ref{chp:proof}, the introduction of the \textbf{Structured Proof (SP)} layer served as a vital cognitive bridge.
While SP is not a logical innovation per se, it acts as a diagnostic tool that decomposes "compressed" folklore arguments into granular steps.
This methodology revealed that many "obvious" steps in the literature require non-trivial formal justification--specifically the measurability of the threshold $\alpha(x)$, a property consistently treated as implicit in pen-and-paper proofs.

\subsection{Representation and Consistency: Permutations versus Sets}
In Chapter \ref{chp:matching}, we encountered the challenge of bridging the \textbf{representation gap} between graph-theoretic permutations and set theoretic edge families.
Formalizing this as an injective mapping was a critical lesson: the correctness of an algorithm depends not only on the logical steps but also on the consistency of data representations.
Without a formal proof of injectivity, the probabilistic bounds could not be safely translated to the bipartite matching model.

\subsection{The Hidden Burden of Rigor: Type Safety and Measurability}
Finally, the reconciliation of type systems (specifically \texttt{nat} and \texttt{int}) and the requirement for measurability proofs provided a rigorous safety layer.
While explicit type casting and measurability obligations added to the proof's complexity, they eliminated the risk of subtle errors.
This underscores that in formal verification, there is no "minor detail", every element of reasoning must be subjected to the highest level of scrutiny.

\section{Future Work and AFP Submission}
The results of this project are currently being prepared for submission to the \textbf{Archive of Formal Proofs (AFP)}.
As a curated collection of Isabelle/HOL developments, the AFP entry will provide a reusable library for the Isolation Lemma, enabling future researchers to verify more complex randomized data structures and algorithms based on the results submitted and archived.

Future research could extend this work in several directions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Verification of Diverse RNC Problems:}
    The algebraic approach used for Perfect Matching can be adapted to other problems in the \textbf{Randomized NC (RNC)} class.
    For instance, the isolation principle is equally vital for parallel algorithms solving the \textit{Matrix Rank} or \textit{All-Pairs Shortest Paths} problems.
    \item \textbf{Expanding the Randomized Algorithm Library}:
    The current formalization can serve as a foundation for verifying other probabilistic building blocks, such as \textbf{Chernoff bounds} \cite{Mitzenmacher_Upfal_2005} or \textbf{Hashing lemmas} \cite{CARTER1979143}, creating a more comprehensive toolkit for randomized algorithm analysis in Isabelle/HOL.
    \item \textbf{Application to Other Combinatorial Structures}:
    While this work focuses on bipartite graphs, the same weighting logic can be tested on simpler combinatorial structures, such as spanning trees or specific subset-sum problems, to evaluate the lemma's versatility.
    \item \textbf{Proof Automation for Probabilistic Reasoning}:
    Future efforts could focus on developing specialized Isabelle strategies to automate tedious formalization tasks, such as measurability proofs and summation index-shifting, reducing the manual effort required for randomized proofs.
\end{itemize}

In conclusion, this work demonstrates that even subtle probabilistic arguments can be subjected to the highest level of logical scrutiny.
By digitalizing these foundational results, we contribute to the ongoing effort of building a verified library of randomized algorithm theory.
