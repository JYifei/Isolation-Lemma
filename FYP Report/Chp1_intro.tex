\section{Background}

Randomized Algorithms plays a fundamental role in theortical computer science.

Unlike deterministic algorithms, randomized algorithms incorporate randomness to break symmetry, simplify theoretical constructions, and achieve improved performance in expectations.
Such algorithms have been widely used in matching problems, parallel computing, approximation algorithms and constructions of complex combinatorial structures.

In many classical results, the introduction of randomized weights serves as a general technique for enforcing certain "uniqueness" or "isolation" properties.
For example, in several parallel computing algorithms and combinatorial optimization problems, algorithmic correctness relies on the fact that, after a random pertubation, a minimum structure becomes unique with high probability.

The Isolation Lemma is a canonical instance of this principle. Informally, this lemma states that when random weight are assigned independently to elements of a finite universe, there is a high probability that a unique minimum-weight set exists within a given family of subsets.
This lemma provides a theortical foundation for numerous randomized algorithms and plays a critical role in algorithmic frameworks for problems such as Perfect Matching.

\section{Motivation}
Although randomized algorithm have achieved significant success in both theory and practice, their correctness often rely on informal probabilistic and combinatorial reasooning.
In traditional pen-and-paper proof, conclusions such as “a minimum obviously exists”, “this event occurs with probability at most 1/N”, or “the relevant events are independent” are frequently treated as self-evident and left implicit.

In a formal proof setting, however, such assumptions cannot be taken for granted. All logical dependencies must be stated explicitly and verified.
For instance, the existence of a minimum depends on the non-emptoness and finiteness of the underlying set; probability statements requires measurability; and independence cannot be asserted informally, but must be derived from the explicit construction of the probability space.

As a consequence, randomized algorithms and their associated lemmas becomes constitute a particularly challenging class of objects for Interactive Theorem Proving(ITP). 
On the one hand, they occupy a central position in theortical computer science; on the other hand, they clearly demonstrate the strength of formal verification in clarifying hidden assumptions and exposing implicit reasoning steps.

This project is motivated by these considerations and aims to reconstruct the complete proof of Isolation Lemma within a fully formalized and rigorous framework.

\section{Project Objectives}
The primary objective of this project is to forrmally model and prove Isolation Lemma formally within the Isabelle/HOL theorem proving system.
This task goes beyond stating the lemma and its probability bound; it also involves formalizing the underlying probability space, defining minimization structures precisely, and providing a structural characterization of non-uniqueness.

Specifically, this project aims to:

Formalize random weight function together with its corresponding product probability space;
Provide a complete formal proof of the Isolation Lemma, including an explicit the upper bound on the probability of non-uniqueness.
Apply the lemma to the correctness analysis of randomized algorithms.

As a concrete application, this project employes the Isolation Lemma to verify a key probabilitic argument in a randomized perfect algorithm for the Perfect Matching problem, thereby illustrating the practical relevance of the lemma within algorithmic theory.

\section{Scope of Work}
The scope of this project in primarily focused on theoretical formalization and proof construction.
In particular, this report covers:

The mathematical formulation of the Isolation Lemma and its formal experssion;
The probabilistic modeling of random weight assignments and their associated properties;
The formalization of critical construction steps within the proof;
A representative application of the lemma to the Perfect Matching Problems.

This work does not address general derandomization techniques for randomized algorithms, nor does it consider implementation-level concerns or performance optimization of specific algorithm.
Moreover, the internal implementation details of the Isabelle/HOL system and its underlying logical foundations is beyond the scope of this report.
