\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chp:intro}

Randomization has become one of the central paradigms of modern theoretical computer science (TCS). 
Over the past few decades, randomized methods have shaped the design and analysis of algorithms, yielding constructions that are simpler, faster, or in some settings seemingly out of reach for known deterministic techniques.

At the heart of randomized algorithms lies a collection of probabilistic lemmas that explain why randomness enforces useful structural properties -- for instance, why a random perturbation can isolate a unique optimum among many candidates.
These lemmas are routinely invoked across areas such as parallel computation, combinatorial optimization and matching.

However, the proofs of such lemmas are often developed as pen-and-paper arguments, or circulate as folklore-style proofs that compress multiple layers of reasoning into a few lines.
In these presentations, assumptions about probability spaces, measurability, independence, and minimization are commonly treated implicitly, and intermediate steps are omitted.

This proof style can be fragile: the intricate details sometimes fail to survive close mathematical scrutiny, especially when probabilistic reasoning interacts with nontrivial algorithmic constructions.
Concrete instances of this phenomenon have appeared in recent work, where closer inspection or formal reconstruction revealed missing conditions or minor errors in widely cited arguments, both in cryptographic settings and in the analysis of advanced algorithmic techniques~\cite{yilei2024quant,kosaianetal2024coppersmith}.

Interactive theorem proving (ITP) provides a disciplined response to this challenge. 
By requiring definitions and proof steps to be stated explicitly and checked mechanically, ITP strengthens rigor, turns informal mathematics into machine-checkable artifacts, and supports large-scale collaboration on proofs.
Recent developments demonstrate that such mechanization can scale to nontrivial randomized algorithm analyses, for example by formally verifying the CVM distinct-elements streaming algorithm in Isabelle/HOL using functional probabilistic invariants to derive concentration bounds~\cite{karayeletal2025CVM}.
Moreover, the resulting formal developments can serve as reusable digital building blocks, contributing to the broader effort of digitalizing mathematics and enabling human--AI collaboration in formal reasoning.

This report contributes to the formal verification of probabilistic reasoning in TCS by formalizing the \emph{Isolation Lemma} in Isabelle/HOL.
The Isolation Lemma is a key tool that assigns random weights to enforce, with high probability, a unique minimum-weight solution structure; it is notably used in randomized algorithms for the \textbf{Perfect Matching} problem.
Our formalization makes the underlying probability space and all dependencies explicit, and we further apply the lemma to support the verification of a randomized perfect matching algorithm. 

\section{Background}
\subsection{Randomized Algorithms in TCS}
Randomized algorithms are a widely studied class of algorithms in theoretical computer science.

Unlike deterministic algorithms, randomized algorithms incorporate randomness to break symmetry, simplify theoretical constructions, and achieve improved expected performance.
Such algorithms have been widely used in matching problems, parallel computing, approximation algorithms, and the construction of complex combinatorial structures.

In many classical results, the introduction of randomized weights serves as a general technique for enforcing certain ``uniqueness'' or ``isolation'' properties.
For example, in several parallel algorithms and combinatorial optimization problems, algorithmic correctness relies on the fact that, after a random perturbation, a minimum structure becomes unique with high probability.

The Isolation Lemma is a canonical instance of this principle. 
Informally, this lemma states that when random weights are assigned independently to elements of a finite universe, there is a high probability that a unique minimum-weight set exists within a given family of subsets.
This lemma provides a theoretical foundation for numerous randomized algorithms and plays a critical role in algorithmic frameworks for problems such as Perfect Matching.

\subsection{Formalization and Theorem Proving}

In theoretical computer science, many fundamental results rely on intricate mathematical arguments and precise logical reasoning.
As theoretical frameworks continue to develop, the scale and complexity of such proofs have grown substantially, making traditional human-based verification increasingly difficult.

Formalized mathematics and interactive theorem proving (ITP) offer an alternative approach to reasoning about mathematical theories.
In this setting, definitions, theorems, and proofs are encoded within a formal logical system, and every inference step is checked rigorously by a computer.
This provides a level of precision and reliability that is difficult to achieve through informal reasoning alone.

A key characteristic of interactive theorem proving is that all assumptions must be stated explicitly.
These include structural properties such as the non-emptiness and finiteness of sets, the totality of functions, and in probabilistic settings, the measurability of events and constructions of probability spaces.
As a consequence, formal proofs do not rely on the intuition of the reader, but on the soundness of the underlying logical framework. 

Beyond individual correctness guarantees, ITP also contributes to the digitalization of mathematical proofs.
Formal developments can be stored, reused, and extended, enabling large-scale collaboration and incremental verification across research communities.
This shift supports new forms of human--computer and human--AI collaboration in mathematics and theoretical computer science.

In recent years, formalized methods have been successfully applied across many areas in computer science, including program verification, compiler correctness, and the formalization of foundational mathematical theories.
Within theoretical computer science, formal verification has gradually expanded from classical mathematical results to core theorems in algorithm theory and computational complexity.

However, the formal verification of randomized algorithms poses additional challenges.
Randomized algorithms combine complex combinatorial reasoning with probabilistic concepts such as independence, distributions, and expectations, all of which must be expressed through explicit constructions rather than being assumed implicitly.
Against this background, the formal verification of key probabilistic lemmas has emerged as an important research direction in interactive theorem proving.

The Isolation Lemma serves as a representative example of such results.
It combines combinatorial minimization with probabilistic reasoning and thus provides an ideal case study for examining how randomized algorithmic arguments can be translated into fully formal, machine-checked proofs.

\section{Project Objectives}
The primary objective of this project is to formally model and prove the Isolation Lemma within the Isabelle/HOL theorem proving system.
This includes establishing its probabilistic statement, associated probability bounds, and the precise conditions under which uniqueness holds.

To achieve this, the project explicitly formalizes the probabilistic and combinatorial structures underlying the lemma, including random weight assignments, independence of random variables, and minimization over finite sets.

Specifically, this project aims to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item formalize random weight functions together with their corresponding product probability spaces, thereby making probabilistic assumptions explicit;
    \item provide a complete machine-checked proof of the Isolation Lemma, including an explicit upper bound on the probability of non-uniqueness and a precise characterization of failure conditions;
    \item apply the formalized lemma to the correctness analysis of randomized algorithms, supporting higher-level algorithmic reasoning.
\end{itemize}

As a concrete application, the Isolation Lemma is used to formally verify a key probabilistic argument in a randomized algorithm for the \textbf{Perfect Matching} problem.

\section{Scope of Work}
The scope of this project is focused on theoretical formalization and proof construction within the Isabelle/HOL framework.
In particular, this report covers:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the mathematical formulation of the Isolation Lemma and its formal expression;
    \item the probabilistic modeling of random weight assignments and their associated properties;
    \item the formalization of key combinatorial and probabilistic arguments appearing in the proof;
    \item a representative application of the lemma to the \textbf{Perfect Matching} problem.
\end{itemize}

This work does not address general derandomization techniques for randomized algorithms, nor does it consider implementation-level concerns or performance optimization.
Moreover, the internal implementation details of the Isabelle/HOL system and its underlying logical foundations are beyond the scope of this report.

\section{Report Outline}

The remainder of this report is organized as follows.

Chapter 2 (Preliminaries) introduces the background material required for this work, including randomized algorithms, probabilistic reasoning, and basic concepts from interactive theorem proving.
This chapter is limited to definitions and results that are not part of the author's original contributions, and serves to establish the theoretical context for the formal developments that follow.

Chapter 3 (Modeling) presents the formal modeling framework used throughout the report.
It describes how the combinatorial and probabilistic structures underlying the Isolation Lemma are represented in Isabelle/HOL, including the construction of probability spaces and auxiliary definitions required for formal reasoning.

Chapter 4 (Proof) contains the core formalization of the Isolation Lemma.
The development is organized using a PP/SP/FP structure -- corresponding to pen-and-paper proofs (PP), structured proofs (SP), and fully formal proofs (FP) -- which is carefully motivated and employed to connect informal probabilistic intuition, precise formal statements, and fully machine-checked formal verification.

Chapter 5 (Perfect Matching) applies the formalized Isolation Lemma to the verification of a randomized algorithm for the Perfect Matching problem.
This chapter focuses on the high-level integration of the lemma into algorithmic reasoning.

Finally, Chapter 6 (Conclusion) summarizes the main contributions of the report and discusses possible directions for future work.
