\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chp:intro}

Randomization is a central paradigm in modern theoretical computer science (TCS) \cite{motwani1995randomized}, and many randomized algorithms rely on probabilistic lemmas that enforce useful structural properties under random perturbation.
One important example is the Isolation Lemma, which shows that a random weight assignment yields, with high probability, a unique minimum-weight solution within a family of sets.

Such arguments are often presented at a pen-and-paper level, with assumptions about probability spaces, independence, measurability, and minimization left implicit.
Formalization provides a way to reconstruct these arguments in a setting where all relevant definitions and proof obligations must be stated explicitly and verified mechanically.

This report studies the formal verification of the Isolation Lemma in Isabelle/HOL and its use in a machine-checked correctness argument for a randomized parallel algorithm for the Perfect Matching problem.
The main contribution is not the underlying algorithmic idea itself, but its mechanized reconstruction:
first, a conditional correctness argument showing that a unique minimum-weight perfect matching can be recovered by algebraic tests,
and second, a probabilistic justification of that uniqueness premise via the formalized Isolation Lemma.

\section{Background}
\subsection{Randomized Algorithms in TCS}
Randomized algorithms form a major class of algorithms in theoretical computer science.
They are often used to break symmetry, simplify constructions, or obtain guarantees that are difficult to achieve deterministically \cite{mulmuley1987matching,vazirani2001approximation}.

In the present report, the relevant randomized technique is random weighting:
random weights are assigned to basic elements so that, with high probability, a unique minimum solution is isolated among many candidates.
This principle is captured by the Isolation Lemma, first introduced by Mulmuley, Vazirani, and Vazirani \cite{mulmuley1987matching}, whose classical statement and proof idea are reviewed in Chapter~\ref{chp:prelims}.

\subsection{Formalization and Theorem Proving}

Formalized mathematics and interactive theorem proving (ITP) provide a framework in which mathematical definitions, statements, and proofs are encoded within a precise logical system and checked mechanically \cite{nipkow2002isabelle}.
In this setting, assumptions that are often left implicit in pen-and-paper proofs must be stated explicitly.

This is particularly relevant for probabilistic arguments such as the Isolation Lemma, where correctness depends not only on combinatorial reasoning but also on explicit probabilistic constructions and side conditions.
In the present report, formalization is used not as an end in itself, but as a way to reconstruct a classical randomized argument in a fully precise and machine-checked form.

\section{Project Objectives}
The primary objective of this project is to formalize and prove the Isolation Lemma within the Isabelle/HOL theorem proving system.
This includes establishing its probabilistic statement, its associated probability bound, and the precise conditions under which uniqueness holds.

To support this development, the project also formalizes the probabilistic and combinatorial structures on which the lemma depends, including random weight assignments, independence of random variables, and minimization over finite sets.

Specifically, this project aims to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item formalize random weight functions together with their corresponding product probability spaces, thereby making probabilistic assumptions explicit;
    \item provide a complete machine-checked proof of the Isolation Lemma, including an explicit upper bound on the probability of non-uniqueness and a precise characterization of failure conditions;
    \item apply the formalized lemma within a machine-checked correctness argument for a randomized parallel algorithm for the Perfect Matching problem.
\end{itemize}

\section{Scope of Work}
The scope of this project is limited to theoretical formalization and proof construction within the Isabelle/HOL framework.
In particular, this report covers:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the mathematical formulation of the Isolation Lemma and its formal expression;
    \item the probabilistic modeling of random weight assignments and their associated properties;
    \item the formalization of key combinatorial and probabilistic arguments used in the proof;
    \item a two-stage machine-checked correctness argument for a randomized parallel algorithm for the \textbf{Perfect Matching} problem, consisting of a conditional correctness proof for the edge extraction and a probabilistic correctness guarantee derived from the formalized Isolation Lemma.
\end{itemize}

This work does not address general derandomization techniques for randomized algorithms, nor does it consider implementation-level concerns or performance optimization.
While the Isolation Lemma is classically associated with randomized parallel approaches to the Perfect Matching problem, the scope of this formalization is limited to verifying the logical and probabilistic correctness of the isolation step.
It does not include the formalization of complexity classes or proofs of complexity-theoretic upper bounds inside Isabelle/HOL.
Moreover, the internal implementation details of the Isabelle/HOL system and its underlying logical foundations are beyond the scope of this report.

\section{Report Outline}

The remainder of this report is organized as follows.

Chapter 2 (Preliminaries) reviews the background material required for this work, including randomized algorithms, probabilistic reasoning, and basic concepts from interactive theorem proving.

Chapter 3 (Modeling) presents the formal modeling framework used throughout the report.
It explains how the combinatorial and probabilistic structures underlying the Isolation Lemma are represented in Isabelle/HOL.

Chapter 4 (Proof) contains the core formalization of the Isolation Lemma.
The development is organized using a PP/SP/FP structure, corresponding to pen-and-paper proofs (PP), structured proofs (SP), and fully formal proofs (FP).

Chapter 5 (Perfect Matching) develops a two-stage machine-checked correctness argument for a randomized parallel algorithm for the Perfect Matching problem.
It combines a conditional algebraic correctness argument with the probabilistic uniqueness guarantee provided by the formalized Isolation Lemma.

Finally, Chapter 6 (Conclusion) summarizes the main contributions of the report and discusses possible directions for future work.
